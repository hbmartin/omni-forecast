# Blending Heterogeneous Weather Forecasts Against a Continuous Ground Truth: A Comparative Landscape

## TL;DR
- **Start with a lead-time- and regime-stratified, performance-weighted mean as your production baseline, then graduate to per-variable statistical postprocessing (EMOS/nonhomogeneous regression, BMA) and gradient-boosted stacking as your accuracy engine — the meteorology literature (anchored by Vannitsem et al. 2021, *BAMS*) repeatedly shows postprocessing that corrects NWP "systematic bias and dispersion errors" beats both the raw providers and a naive average while also yielding calibrated distributions.** With fewer than 10 providers and lots of history, "few models, richly featured" beats "many crudely weighted models."
- **Solve the resolution mismatch by scoring every provider at its own native cadence (aggregate per-minute truth up to hourly/daily to score coarse forecasts), then reconcile the separately-produced minute/hour/day blends with temporal-hierarchy reconciliation (thief/MinT) so they agree — but note that daily high/low (max/min) forecasts break the linear-summing assumption of standard reconciliation and need special handling.**
- **For robustness to concept drift and provider dropout, prefer methods with re-normalizable/estimable weights on rolling or exponentially-forgetting windows, or online expert-aggregation (EWA/BOA/ML-Poly) which tolerate "sleeping" (missing) experts by design and carry regret guarantees; verify everything with proper scoring rules (CRPS, log score, pinball loss) under rolling-origin cross-validation.**

## Key Findings

1. **The equal-weighted mean is the benchmark to beat, not a strawman.** Across the M-competitions and decades of literature, the simple arithmetic average of forecasts routinely outperforms the best individual model and even "optimal" estimated-weight schemes — the "forecast combination puzzle," coined by Stock and Watson (2004). As Wang and Hyndman's 50-year review puts it, simple averaging "often empirically dominates sophisticated weighting schemes... a tough benchmark, with few combination schemes outperforming it." The dominant explanation is estimation error: optimal weights require estimating an error covariance matrix that is poorly identified in finite samples, so the estimated weights add variance without adding enough signal. This is the single most important framing fact for the whole project.

2. **Your advantage — few providers, lots of history — is exactly the regime where beating the mean is achievable.** The puzzle bites hardest when the number of forecasts is large relative to sample size. With <10 providers and a long observation record, you can reliably estimate lead-time-, regime-, and season-conditional skill, which is precisely the kind of structure a plain mean throws away.

3. **This is fundamentally a weather postprocessing problem, and that literature is more directly applicable than generic econometric combination.** EMOS/nonhomogeneous regression, Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA), quantile regression forests, and neural distributional regression were all built to (a) calibrate per-variable, (b) handle lead-time-dependent skill, and (c) emit predictive distributions — three of your explicit requirements. They also handle the "bias + dispersion" correction that raw providers need.

4. **The resolution mismatch has two complementary solutions, and you likely want both.** Aggregating per-minute truth up to the provider's cadence is the correct way to *score and weight* coarse providers. Temporal-hierarchy reconciliation (thief + MinT) is the correct way to make your *separately-produced* minute/hour/day blends mutually coherent. These are not competitors; scoring-up feeds the weighting, reconciliation harmonizes the outputs.

5. **Daily high/low ranges are the genuinely hard modeling wrinkle.** Max and min are non-linear operators, so they violate the linear-summing assumption behind standard temporal reconciliation. Treat daily extremes as a joint distribution (EMOS + copula) or as within-day quantiles, rather than forcing them into a linear coherence constraint.

## Details

### A. Forecast combination / ensemble methods

**The classical foundation.** Bates and Granger (1969) launched the field by showing a composite of two forecasts can have lower mean-square error than either component, with weights derived from past errors. The variance-minimizing "optimal" weight, ignoring correlations, is the inverse-error-variance rule wᵢ ∝ σ̂ᵢ⁻² / Σⱼ σ̂ⱼ⁻². Newbold and Granger (1974) extended this to many forecasts; Granger and Ramanathan (1984) reframed combination as linear regression, showing that regressing the target on the forecasts with an **unconstrained intercept and freely-estimated weights** produces an unbiased combination even when the constituents are biased (their "method A" / FC1). Constraining weights to sum to one and dropping the intercept recovers the Bates–Granger optimal weights and eliminates serially-correlated combination errors — a practical reason many practitioners keep the sum-to-one constraint.

**The forecast combination puzzle.** Stock and Watson (2004) coined the term for the repeated empirical finding that equal weights beat estimated-optimal weights. Claeskens et al. (2016) gave the standard theoretical explanation: because weights are estimated, the "optimal" combination is actually a biased, high-variance estimator; Smith and Wallis (2009) attribute the effect to estimation error in the weights. The practical takeaway (Timmermann 2006): correlations between forecast errors are hard to estimate, so schemes that ignore covariances (inverse-MSE weights) generally beat schemes that try to estimate the full covariance matrix. Qian, Rolling, Cheng and Yang (2016, "On the Forecast Combination Puzzle") add the useful distinction between "combining for adaptation" vs. "combining for improvement," arguing that mismatching your method to the scenario itself causes the puzzle.

**Bias–variance and many-vs-few.** The variance-reduction from averaging K unbiased forecasts scales down with the number of models but is capped by their error correlation: perfectly correlated models give no diversification benefit; the gain comes from diversity, not count. With <10 providers, you are firmly in the regime where a small number of well-characterized, diverse providers combined with a few estimated parameters is preferable to a many-model scheme whose weights you cannot estimate stably. Simple robust variants — trimmed mean, median, Winsorized mean (all implemented in R's `GeomComb`/`ForecastComb`) — hedge against a single bad provider at almost no cost and are a sensible upgrade from the plain mean.

**Time-varying and regime-switching weights.** Because provider skill drifts and is regime-dependent, static weights are suboptimal. Bates and Granger themselves used rolling windows and exponential discounting. Diebold and Pauly (1987) generalized adaptive weighting; Deutsch, Granger and Teräsvirta (1994) used smooth-transition weights; Elliott and Timmermann (2005, "Optimal Forecast Combination Under Regime Switching," *International Economic Review* 46:1081–1102) let combination weights follow a latent Markov-switching state — directly relevant to your "conditions/regime" requirement. Time-varying weighting is noted as one of the few schemes that reliably escapes the combination puzzle, precisely because the optimal weights genuinely are time-varying.

### B. Weather-specific statistical postprocessing

This is the literature closest to your problem, and I recommend anchoring the project on it. The authoritative survey is **Vannitsem et al. (2021), "Statistical Postprocessing for Weather Forecasts: Review, Challenges, and Avenues in a Big Data World," *BAMS* 102(3):E681–E699** — a 24-author review from the operational meteorology community that frames postprocessing as correcting NWP "systematic bias and dispersion errors," and explicitly lists "the necessity for techniques to merge different types of forecasts and ensembles with different behaviors" and "the need to reduce the impact of model changes affecting the parameters of the corrections" (i.e., your concept-drift concern) as central open challenges.

**MOS (Model Output Statistics).** The classical approach: regress observed weather on model output to correct systematic bias. Deutscher Wetterdienst's operational Ensemble-MOS derives predictands (temperature, precipitation, wind gusts) from synoptic observations via linear/logistic regression over long training series — a direct template for regressing your ground truth on provider outputs.

**EMOS / nonhomogeneous Gaussian regression (Gneiting et al. 2005).** The workhorse. It fits a parametric predictive distribution whose parameters are functions of the ensemble/providers. For temperature the predictive distribution is Gaussian with mean μ = a + b·x̄ (affine in the ensemble/provider mean) and variance σ² = c² + d²·s² (affine in the ensemble variance), with coefficients estimated by **minimizing CRPS** over a rolling training window (Gneiting et al. used 30–40 days). It simultaneously corrects bias and dispersion. Variable-specific distributions exist: Gaussian for temperature/pressure; truncated normal, log-normal, and censored/heteroscedastic regression for wind speed (Thorarinsdottir and Gneiting 2010; Baran and Lerch 2015); censored shifted gamma / GEV for precipitation (Scheuerer 2014). **EMOS gives you exactly the calibrated distribution "nice-to-have," per-variable, at each lead time.**

**Bayesian Model Averaging (Raftery, Gneiting, Balabdaoui and Polakowski 2005, *MWR* 133:1155–1174).** The BMA predictive PDF is a weighted average of bias-corrected component PDFs (one per provider), with weights equal to each provider's posterior probability of being best over a training period, estimated by EM. In the original study, "90% BMA prediction intervals were 66% shorter on average than those produced by sample climatology" while remaining calibrated (91.7% coverage) — and, critically for you, **BMA yields a deterministic point forecast as a by-product, with root-mean-square errors 7% lower than the best of the ensemble members and 8% lower than the ensemble mean.** BMA maps almost perfectly onto your problem: "PDF centered on each bias-corrected provider, weighted by historical reliability." Weights are non-negative and sum to one, so provider dropout is handled by re-normalizing over available providers.

**Quantile regression and quantile regression forests (Taillardat, Mestre, Zamo and Naveau 2016, *MWR* 144:2375–2393).** QRF is a nonparametric alternative that makes no distributional assumption — it uses random-forest leaf memberships to build an empirical predictive distribution and read off any quantile. It naturally ingests extra predictors (lead time, hour-of-day, other weather variables) and is **used operationally at Météo-France**. Its limitation: predicted quantiles are bounded by the training range (bad for unprecedented extremes), addressed by tail-extended variants (TQRF, Taillardat et al. 2019). QRF often beats even neural methods at specific locations due to local adaptivity.

**Analog methods** find historically similar forecast situations and use the observed outcomes as the predictive ensemble — conceptually the nonparametric limit of the same idea, and the intuition underlying QRF's leaf analogs.

### C. Machine-learning approaches

**Gradient-boosted trees as a stacking meta-learner.** Train XGBoost/LightGBM to map [provider forecasts + engineered features] → ground truth. The killer feature for you is that engineered features — lead time, hour-of-day (cyclical), day-of-year/season, a regime indicator, provider agreement/spread — let a single model encode all three of your skill-variation requirements (lead time, regime, cyclicality) without separate per-stratum models. Trees natively handle missing inputs (provider dropout) via default directions and are robust to monotone rescaling. `MultiOutputRegressor` wrappers predict several weather variables jointly. This is the pragmatic sweet spot: interpretable-ish (feature importances, SHAP), cheap to retrain (robustness to drift), and strong with tabular data.

**FFORMA (Montero-Manso, Athanasopoulos, Hyndman and Talagala 2020, *IJF* 36(1):86–92).** The canonical feature-based combination: an XGBoost meta-learner maps time-series features to combination weights over base models by minimizing the combined forecast loss via a custom objective (softmax-transformed weights, custom gradient/hessian). Per the authors, "FFORMA resulted in the second most accurate point forecasts and prediction intervals amongst all competitors" in the M4 competition. For your setting, "features" would be lead time, regime, and seasonal descriptors, and the "base models" are your providers — this is essentially the principled version of regime-conditional weighting.

**Neural distributional regression (Rasp and Lerch 2018, *MWR* 146:3885–3900).** A neural network outputs the parameters of a predictive distribution, trained by minimizing CRPS directly. It learns nonlinear predictor→parameter links automatically (no pre-specified link functions), uses station embeddings for local adaptivity, and per the authors "the neural network models significantly outperform state-of-the-art post-processing techniques while being computationally more efficient" (German 2-m temperature case study; code public at `slerch/ppnn`). Sequence models (LSTMs, temporal convolutional networks, temporal fusion transformers) extend this where temporal dynamics matter, but they demand more data and tuning; with <10 providers and tabular structure, boosted trees usually match or beat them at far lower cost.

**Distributional/probabilistic ML output options:** quantile ("pinball") loss for direct quantile estimation; NGBoost for probabilistic gradient boosting; conformal prediction as a distribution-free wrapper that turns any point or quantile forecaster into intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees under exchangeability. Because weather series are non-exchangeable/drifting, use time-series-adapted conformal variants: Adaptive Conformal Inference (ACI; Gibbs and Candès 2021), EnbPI (Xu and Xie 2021), or Bellman Conformal Inference, which maintain long-run coverage under distribution shift. Conformal calibration is a cheap, model-agnostic way to earn the "calibrated interval" nice-to-have on top of any point blend.

### D. Hierarchical and temporal reconciliation

**The core idea.** A temporal hierarchy is built from a single series by non-overlapping temporal aggregation — no new data needed (Athanasopoulos, Hyndman, Kourentzes and Petropoulos 2017, "Forecasting with Temporal Hierarchies," *EJOR* 262(1):60–74). For a series with m observations per cycle, the aggregation levels are the **integer factors of m** (k₁=1 finest up to k_p=m coarsest). Each k-aggregate is a non-overlapping sum of k successive values. This produces the "thief" (Temporal HIErarchical Forecasting) workflow: forecast independently at every granularity, then reconcile.

**MinT reconciliation (Wickramasuriya, Athanasopoulos and Hyndman 2019, *JASA* 114(526):804–819).** The state-of-the-art. Given base forecasts ŷ at all levels and a summing matrix S (0/1 entries encoding parent-child aggregation, structured S = [C; Iₘ]), MinT finds coherent forecasts minimizing the trace of the reconciled-error covariance:
ỹ = S(SᵀW⁻¹S)⁻¹SᵀW⁻¹ ŷ.
The choice of W matters: OLS (W=I); WLS (diagonal of base-error variances); **structural scaling** (W ∝ diag of row-sums of S — needs no estimation, the default `struc` in thief); sample covariance (`sam`); and **MinT-shrink** (shrinkage toward diagonal — recommended when the number of bottom nodes is large relative to series length). Reconciliation shares information across scales and generally improves the mean forecast — though there is **no guarantee of improvement at every individual level**, and estimating W for h>1 is genuinely hard. `reconcilethief(forecasts, comb=c("struc","mse","ols","bu","shr","sam"))` implements all of this in R's `thief`; cross-sectional grouped hierarchies live in `hts`/`fable`.

**Mapping your minute→hour→day case.** The framework admits only aggregation levels that are exact integer factors of the base frequency. Minute→hour→day works cleanly because 60 and 1440 are integer multiples (1 min → 60 min → 1440 min; use factors of 1440). Levels that aren't integer factors can't sit in one clean hierarchy — pick a base grid divisible by all target periods. A starting offset t* = T − ⌊T/m⌋·m + 1 aligns aggregates and avoids ragged edges.

**Cross-temporal reconciliation (the full solution).** Because you have multiple variables AND multiple temporal levels, the relevant frontier is cross-temporal reconciliation, which imposes temporal and cross-sectional constraints simultaneously. The point-forecast method is Di Fonzo and Girolimetto (2023), "Cross-temporal forecast reconciliation: Optimal combination method and heuristic alternatives," *IJF* 39(1):39–57. The probabilistic extension is **Girolimetto, Athanasopoulos, Di Fonzo and Hyndman (2024), "Cross-temporal probabilistic forecast reconciliation: Methodological and practical issues," *IJF* 40(3):1134–1151** (DOI 10.1016/j.ijforecast.2023.10.003). Their method draws from the incoherent joint distribution via parametric-Gaussian or non-parametric joint-bootstrap sampling, uses **multi-step (h-step) residuals** — a key practical point, since one-step residuals fail in the temporal dimension — and offers four covariance estimators exploiting the two-fold structure. On Australian GDP and Tourism data, cross-temporal reconciliation significantly beat incoherent base forecasts on both **CRPS and Energy Score**. Both are implemented in the **`FoReco` R package** (Girolimetto and Di Fonzo, on CRAN): `octrec()` for optimal-combination cross-temporal reconciliation, `iterec()` for the iterative heuristic, plus point and probabilistic reconciliation across cross-sectional, temporal, and cross-temporal frameworks.

**Handling daily high/low ranges — the non-linear wrinkle.** Standard reconciliation assumes the aggregation operator is SUM (or mean, a linear rescaling). Daily max/min are **non-linear** operators, so they cannot be placed in a standard S-matrix. Three routes:
1. **Non-linearly constrained reconciliation (NLCR)** — Girolimetto, Panagiotelis, Di Fonzo and Li (2025, arXiv:2510.21249, a working paper not yet peer-reviewed) generalizes reconciliation to projection onto a non-linear coherent manifold {y : g(y)=0}, solved by sequential quadratic programming (SLSQP via `nloptr` in R or `scipy.optimize` in Python; package `nlcReco`). Crucial caveat: unlike the linear case, **there is no guarantee of error reduction for non-linear constraints**, and reliability degrades with constraint curvature — and max/min have extreme curvature (non-differentiable kinks), the worst case. No published application to block-maxima yet exists; this is a genuine literature gap.
2. **Joint-distribution modeling (recommended, and the meteorology-standard route).** Model daily Tmax and Tmin as a joint bivariate distribution via EMOS on the marginals + Ensemble Copula Coupling for the dependence (see the *IJF* paper "Comparing probabilistic forecasts of the daily minimum and maximum temperature"). Score against the realized daily max/min of your per-minute truth.
3. **Quantile proxy (pragmatic middle path).** Treat the daily high as a high quantile and the daily low as a low quantile of the within-day minute distribution, letting you reuse your quantile machinery without a hard non-linear constraint.

### E. Handling practical issues

**Provider dropout / missing forecasts.** Ranked by robustness: (i) methods with re-normalizable weights (BMA, inverse-error weights, online aggregation) simply renormalize over available providers; (ii) tree ensembles tolerate missing inputs natively; (iii) regression-combination and neural methods need imputation or missingness indicators. A Bayesian imputation framework with exponentially-weighted likelihood has been proposed specifically for missing analyst/provider forecasts. Online expert aggregation handles this most elegantly via "sleeping experts" — experts abstain when absent and rejoin without breaking the regret guarantee.

**Concept drift / non-stationarity.** Options in increasing sophistication: rolling or expanding windows; exponential forgetting/discounting; explicit drift detection (ADWIN); and **online expert aggregation** — Exponentially Weighted Average (EWA; Vovk 1990, Littlestone and Warmuth 1994), Bernstein Online Aggregation (BOA; Wintenberger 2017), Fixed-Share (Herbster and Warmuth 1998), and ML-Poly (Gaillard et al. 2014) — all with sublinear-regret guarantees relative to the best expert or best sequence of experts, implemented in R's **`opera`** package. Directly relevant precedent: **Pfitzner, Wintenberger, Mestre and Riverain (2025), "Contribution of expert aggregation to temperature prediction" (Parts I & II, arXiv:2506.15217 / 2506.15216)**, from Météo-France, apply exactly these methods to combine NWP/MOS temperature forecasts. They state expert aggregation "has many advantages, such as being online, being adaptive to model changes and having theoretical guarantees," and Part II uses a Sleeping-Expert Framework on BOA to handle changing environments and occasionally-useful extreme-event (biased-quantile) experts. This is arguably the single closest published analog to your problem. Note the documented tension: sliding windows and fast adaptation improve responsiveness to drift and let the method exploit occasionally-useful biased experts, but reduce average-case performance — a real tradeoff to tune.

**Verification and scoring.** Use **proper scoring rules** (Gneiting and Raftery 2007): the **CRPS** (strictly proper, in the units of the variable, rewards calibration + sharpness jointly, reduces to MAE for point forecasts) and the **log/ignorance score** for full distributions; **pinball/quantile loss** for quantile forecasts; the **Winkler interval score** for prediction intervals; MAE/RMSE for point blends; **skill scores** relative to baselines (climatology, persistence, the equal-weighted mean); and **reliability diagrams**/PIT histograms for calibration. R's `scoringRules` (Jordan, Krüger and Lerch) and Python's `properscoring` implement CRPS and friends. To fairly compare providers with different horizons/resolutions, **score each at its native cadence against truth aggregated to that cadence**, and always report skill relative to a common baseline so a 3-day provider and a 10-day provider are judged on overlapping horizons only.

**Cross-validation for time series.** Never use random k-fold. Use **rolling-origin / time-series cross-validation** (expanding or sliding training windows with strictly forward test folds), a.k.a. blocked evaluation, so the temporal ordering and drift are respected and leakage is avoided.

## Recommendations

**Stage 0 — Infrastructure and scoring (do first, non-negotiable).** Build the evaluation harness before any modeling: align all providers to a common time index; aggregate per-minute ground truth up to hourly and daily (mean for level variables; realized max/min for range targets). Implement CRPS, MAE/RMSE, pinball loss, and skill-vs-baseline, all under rolling-origin CV. Compute a **skill matrix per provider × variable × lead-time bucket × season/regime** — this both diagnoses your providers and directly parameterizes the weighting in later stages. *Benchmark to beat throughout: the equal-weighted mean and the single best provider per stratum.*

**Stage 1 — Robust weighted-mean baseline.** Deploy a **lead-time- and regime-stratified inverse-error-weighted mean** (Bates–Granger inverse-MSE weights, computed within strata on a rolling/exponentially-forgetting window), with a trimmed-mean or median fallback for robustness. Re-normalize weights over available providers to handle dropout. This is cheap, transparent, drift-robust, and per the combination-puzzle literature is the honest bar every fancier method must clear. *Move on only if Stage 2/3 beat this out-of-sample on CRPS/MAE by a margin that survives rolling-origin CV.*

**Stage 2 — Per-variable statistical postprocessing (the accuracy + distribution engine).** For each variable and native cadence, fit **EMOS/nonhomogeneous regression** (Gaussian for temperature; truncated-normal or censored regression for wind speed) with CRPS estimation on rolling windows, and/or **BMA** (gives calibrated PDF + a point forecast as by-product, with natural dropout handling). Add **QRF** where you have rich auxiliary predictors and want a nonparametric, locally-adaptive alternative. These deliver the calibrated-distribution nice-to-have and lead-time-specific calibration simultaneously.

**Stage 3 — Gradient-boosted stacking (the flexible meta-learner).** Train LightGBM/XGBoost mapping [provider forecasts + lead time + cyclical hour/season encodings + regime features + provider spread] → ground truth, one model per variable (or multi-output), using quantile loss for distributional output. This subsumes the stratified weighting of Stage 1 into a single learned model, encodes all three skill-variation axes, tolerates missing providers, and is cheap to retrain against drift. FFORMA-style feature-based weighting is the principled variant if you prefer explicit interpretable weights. Wrap the output in **time-series conformal prediction (ACI/EnbPI)** for coverage-guaranteed intervals.

**Stage 4 — Drift/dropout hardening.** If provider model changes turn out to be frequent, layer **online expert aggregation (`opera`: BOA or ML-Poly, with Fixed-Share/sleeping-experts)** over your Stage 1–3 blends-as-experts. The Météo-France temperature-aggregation papers are your implementation template. This gives adaptive, guarantee-backed weighting that degrades gracefully.

**Stage 5 — Temporal coherence.** Produce minute/hour/day blends separately (Stages 2–3), then reconcile them with **thief/MinT** (start with structural scaling `struc`, upgrade to `shr` if you have the data) so the scales agree, or go straight to **cross-temporal reconciliation via `FoReco` (`octrec`/`iterec`)** to reconcile across variables and scales jointly, with the probabilistic variant for distributional coherence. **Handle daily high/low outside the linear reconciliation** — model them as a joint EMOS+copula distribution or as within-day quantiles; only experiment with NLCR if you accept it carries no error-reduction guarantee.

**Thresholds that change the plan.** (a) If Stage 1 is never beaten on a given variable/horizon after honest rolling-origin CV, ship Stage 1 there and stop. (b) If provider error correlations are high (>~0.9), skip regression/covariance-based weighting entirely and stay with inverse-MSE or shrinkage. (c) If drift is detected frequently (weights or residual bias shifting materially between adjacent CV folds), prioritize Stage 4 over Stage 3 complexity. (d) If calibrated intervals become a hard requirement rather than a nice-to-have, EMOS/BMA/QRF (Stage 2) move ahead of the boosted point-stacker.

**Where "many models" vs. "few models" lands for you:** With <10 providers and lots of history, favor **few models, richly featured**: a handful of diverse providers combined through one well-regularized, feature-rich meta-model (boosted stacker or EMOS/BMA) beats a large bank of crudely-weighted sub-models whose weights you cannot estimate stably. Reserve "many models" thinking for the *feature/regime stratification* (many conditional weightings inside one model), not for multiplying base learners.

## Caveats
- **The combination puzzle is real and humbling.** Expect that on some variables/horizons the equal-weighted mean or a lightly-trimmed mean will not be beaten by anything fancier out-of-sample. Keep it as a permanent baseline and only ship complexity that demonstrably beats it under rolling-origin CV.
- **Reconciliation improves the whole but not necessarily each part.** MinT minimizes total trace error; individual levels can get worse, and estimating the error covariance for multi-step horizons is hard. Validate level-by-level.
- **Non-linear reconciliation of max/min is an open research area, not a solved tool.** The NLCR method (arXiv:2510.21249) and the FoReco/FoRecoML non-linear toolbox are recent, non-peer-reviewed preprints; max/min's extreme curvature is the worst case for the method's (already unguaranteed) error reduction. Prefer joint-distribution or quantile treatments of ranges.
- **Provider forecasts may be mutually dependent and may themselves already be blends** (many consumer weather APIs post-process the same underlying NWP models). High error correlation caps the diversification benefit of any combination and can destabilize covariance estimation — another argument for correlation-ignoring inverse-MSE weights or shrinkage.
- **Distribution shift breaks conformal coverage guarantees.** Vanilla split conformal assumes exchangeability, which weather drift violates; use the adaptive time-series variants and monitor realized coverage.
- **Scoring across heterogeneous horizons must be like-for-like.** Comparing a provider's 10-day skill against another's 3-day skill is meaningless; always restrict comparisons to overlapping horizons and report skill relative to a shared baseline.
